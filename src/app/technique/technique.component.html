Having good technique is crucial when it comes to making precise shots. Even with a perfectly straight cue ball, many players struggle to hit their target when they start to feather the ball. One way to improve this is by focusing on two fixed points while feathering. This helps to maintain a straight line and improve accuracy.

- The first key element in maintaining a straight line while feathering is the bridge. It's important to keep in mind that it can only serve as a fixed point if it stays steady and doesn't move.
- Another important fixed point to consider is the body. By keeping a steady and consistent posture, the body can serve as a reference point to ensure that the cue stick is moving in a straight line.

To enhance the stability of the guidance cue, we can add two additional fixed points. To do this, we can lean over the table and rotate our shoulders by 90 degrees, while also contracting our chest and chin towards the cue. Initially, we should position the cue on the firing line and then allow our body to lean against the cue. This will simplify the process of guiding the cue while feathering.


A common mistake among players is to move the cue towards their body when attempting to lean on it. This causes the cue to no longer be aligned with the line of fire, which can negatively impact accuracy. Instead, it's important to maintain the proper positioning of the cue while leaning on it to ensure proper shot alignment.

The location at which we grip the cue can greatly affect the movement of the pointer during feathering. Holding the cue too close to the end can result in limited movement and difficulty in adjusting for accuracy. On the other hand, gripping the cue too far away from the white ball can also cause a short movement of the cue and affect the accuracy of the shot. It is important to find a balance between the distance of the grip from the end of the cue and the distance from the white ball for optimal feathering.

Remember: When taking a shot, it is important to not lean the cue against your body, but instead, let your body lean against the cue. This will ensure that your shot is stable and accurate. By keeping your body in the proper position and allowing it to support the cue, you can reduce the chances of any unwanted movements that can affect the accuracy of your shot.

Forming a proper bridge is important for accurate shots. Here's how to do it:
1. Place your palm flat on the table, spreading your fingers to the desired width.
2. Carefully lift your knuckles by pulling the pads of your fingers across the table, making sure that the entire pads are resting on the surface.
3. Keep your fingers straight as you slide your thumb into the middle of your fingers and lock it onto your index finger. This will provide stability for the bridge.
4. The final result should be an indentation that resembles a letter V, through which the cue can pass. This will provide a stable base for the cue and allow for precise shots.

The cue action is the deliberate and controlled movement of the cue in order to make the shot. It is important to keep the cue action calm, so that your motor skills are relaxed and the shot is executed smoothly. This will help to ensure that the cue ball is hit as precisely as possible.

Why the pause?

The pause before the shot is crucial to maintaining control and accuracy. Without a pause, the cue may accelerate too quickly upon impact, causing the shot to be executed poorly. This can lead to a lack of control and difficulty in keeping the cue in a straight line. Pausing before the shot will help to maintain control and ensure a smooth and accurate execution.

Where do you look when hitting the white ball? When striking the white ball, you should look at the ball that you are trying to hit. It is not recommended to look at the ball that has just been played (the cue ball) as this can affect your aim and accuracy.

This can be done in three ways:

The first way - We prepare to strike and look at the white ball and the ball being played, keeping our eyes focused on the cue as it moves back and forth. We make sure to watch the cue as it moves along the line of impact. This method allows us to see how the cue is moving and adjust the strike if necessary. However, it takes more focus to keep our eyes on the cue instead of the ball being played.

The second way - We prepare to strike by keeping our eyes on the white ball and the ball to be struck. We focus on the point of impact and make the strike without looking at the cue as it retreats. This method allows for quick and precise strikes, but it can be difficult to gauge the power of the shot without visually tracking the cue.

The third way - We prepare to strike by looking at the cue ball and aiming, then we focus on the cue as it moves back and forth. This method allows for precision and control of the cue, as well as giving us ample time to focus on the ball being played.

Hitting balls straight on is relatively easy when our technique is sound, but when it comes to pocketing at specific angles, things can become challenging. One of the most common dilemmas faced by novice snooker players is how to properly aim and strike the cue ball in order to pocket it. The key is to understand how to recognize the correct angles and apply the right technique to ensure the ball falls into the pocket. This requires practice and a good understanding of geometry and the physics of the game. Learning how to read and utilize the angles on the table is crucial in becoming a skilled snooker player.

If we have a shot where the cue ball is at an angle to both the object ball and the pocket, we can approach the shot in two different ways.

The first way One approach to hitting angled shots is to practice striking the cue ball at different points on its surface. This can include hitting the ball full-on, at a 3/4 position, a 1/2 position, or a 1/4 position. Through practice, you will develop the ability to recognize the precise point of contact and make small adjustments in your stroke to pocket the ball more accurately.


The second way Another method for hitting angled shots is to visualize an imaginary "ghost ball" that is tangent to the object ball and in line with the pocket. Stand in line with this ghost ball, and use it as a guide for your shot. Focus on the center of the ghost ball as you set up and take your shot, without looking away from the ghost ball. This can help you aim more accurately and increase your chances of pocketing the object ball.



A combination of both of these methods can be the most effective way to improve your ability to hit angled shots. Practice hitting the ball from different angles, and before each shot, take a moment to visualize the ghost ball. This approach allows you to receive feedback from both your physical practice and your visualized shots. Over time, this routine and repetition will help you develop a sense of the correct angle for a shot both physically and mentally. The key is to keep up a consistent routine, as this is how you will develop muscle memory and improve your ability to recognize and execute angled shots.

TOP SPIN, BACK SPIN, LEFT SIDE, RIGHT SIDE
In order to apply spin to the cue ball in basic shots, you should focus on striking the ball at different points along its circumference. When practicing these strokes, it is important to pay attention to the angle of your cue stick and the speed at which you strike the ball.

To master snooker, it is essential to master three basic types of strokes. These include:
1. Hitting the ball with progressive spin, which will cause the cue ball to move forward while rotating.
2. Hitting the ball without any spin, which will cause the cue ball to move in a straight path without rotation.
3. Hitting the ball with backward spin, which will cause the cue ball to move backwards while rotating.

It is important to practice these strokes regularly and to pay attention to the specifics of each stroke in order to improve your overall game.

IMPORTANT:

When applying spin to the cue ball, it is important to ensure that the tip of your cue stick is properly chalked. This will help to increase friction between the cue tip and the ball, allowing for more control and precision when applying spin. Additionally, check that the chalk is not too old or worn out, as this can also affect the grip of the cue tip on the ball.

Top spin One way to apply a forward spin to the cue ball is to use a "top spin" shot. To execute this shot, you should raise the bridge of your hand and strike the top of the cue ball. This causes the cue ball to rotate forward and move forward after striking the object ball. The cue ball will roll forward behind the object ball. It's important to practice this shot to get the right touch and feel of the cue ball and object ball interaction.


Stop ball Another basic shot is hitting the cue ball dead center, without any spin. To execute this shot, you will need to slightly lower the bridge of your hand, and aim for the center of the cue ball. This will cause the cue ball to slide down the cloth and come into contact with the object ball, while the white ball remains in the same spot. This shot is important to master as it will allow you to control the cue ball's speed and trajectory more precisely.


Back spin A third basic shot is the "back spin" shot. To execute this shot, you will need to place the bridge of your hand flat on the table, and strike the cue ball from a downward angle. This will cause the cue ball to rotate backwards and move backwards after striking the object ball. This shot is useful when you need to play a safety shot and keep the cue ball away from the pocket and also push the object ball away from the pocket. It takes practice to master the right angle and power to achieve the desired backward rotation on the cue ball.


IMPORTANT:

When applying back spin, it is crucial to ensure that the cue is guided in a parallel manner.
If parallelism and stability are not maintained while guiding the cue, the white ball may jump.
