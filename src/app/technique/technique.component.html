After determining the line of fire and folding along the impact we proceed to the cue action. Cue action should proceed in a smooth and calm manner without any quick and sudden movements. The number of repetitions should always be the same same. Before the stroke itself, there must be followed by a momentary pause.

Why the cue action?

The cue action is the fixation in muscles of rectilinear movement along which we want to make the shot. The cue action must be calm in order to calm our motor skills so that the hit is derived as calmly as possible.

Why the pause?

The pause is very important because if you don't pause the blow will break because the cue will accelerate too much to the forward which will make it very difficult to bring the cue out in a straight line.

The course of the cue action on the example of three lashings:

1. First extraction
2. Second extraction
3. Third extraction
4. Fourth pullback and first pause
5. Smoothly bringing the cue forward with appropriate dynamics
6. The strike is followed by a natural deceleration of the cue and a second pause
7. In this position after the shot we stay for a while

Thanks to the cue action, we can select the strength of the impact. If I want to play a light stroke my feathering should have short lashings, while if I want to play a strong shot of the blow should be longer.

Where do you look when hitting the white ball? When striking, do you look at the at the white ball or at the played ball? The answer is only one to the ball played.

This can be done in three ways:

The first way - We prepare to strike and look at the white ball and the ball being played, eyes hovering between them along the line of impact. We stare at the the ball being played we pull back the cue and strike. This method gives us plenty of time to focus on the ball we want to pocket. The disadvantage is that we can't see how the cue is retreating and we can pull the cue back a little too hard.

The second way - We prepare to strike by looking at the ball the white ball and the ball to be struck. We stop looking at the cue ball and look at the ball being played when the cue retreats and we strike. In this method we have less time to focus on the ball the ball being played.

The third way - We prepare to strike by looking at the cue ball, aiming. I look at the cue pulling it back and stopping it. Still we look at the cue before the actual strike I look at the ball being played and strike. This method just like the first one gives you more time to focus on the ball being played.

Where to position the dominant eye and where to hold the head during impact?

When the players fold up to hit and start the cue action, you may notice that some players cue more under the right or left eye. This is because they have one eye more dominant, so if the right eye is dominant cue will be more under the right eye.

So how to determine which eye is dominant eye, first put the tickets on the table. Take a piece of piece of paper and cut a hole in it. Hold the piece of paper with your straight hands, and then look through the hole at the ball. Then close your left eye if you can still see the ball right eye you have, the dominance of the of the right eye. Again verify, straighten your arms and look at the ball through the cut hole in the card and close right eye. If the ball disappears it again proves that indeed your right eye is dominant. If you have dominance of the left eye the situation is reversed.
