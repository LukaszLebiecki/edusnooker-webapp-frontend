Having good technique is crucial when it comes to making precise shots. Even with a perfectly straight cue ball, many players struggle to hit their target when they start to feather the ball. One way to improve this is by focusing on two fixed points while feathering. This helps to maintain a straight line and improve accuracy.

- The first key element in maintaining a straight line while feathering is the bridge. It's important to keep in mind that it can only serve as a fixed point if it stays steady and doesn't move.
- Another important fixed point to consider is the body. By keeping a steady and consistent posture, the body can serve as a reference point to ensure that the cue stick is moving in a straight line.

To enhance the stability of the guidance cue, we can add two additional fixed points. To do this, we can lean over the table and rotate our shoulders by 90 degrees, while also contracting our chest and chin towards the cue. Initially, we should position the cue on the firing line and then allow our body to lean against the cue. This will simplify the process of guiding the cue while feathering.


A common mistake among players is to move the cue towards their body when attempting to lean on it. This causes the cue to no longer be aligned with the line of fire, which can negatively impact accuracy. Instead, it's important to maintain the proper positioning of the cue while leaning on it to ensure proper shot alignment.

The location at which we grip the cue can greatly affect the movement of the pointer during feathering. Holding the cue too close to the end can result in limited movement and difficulty in adjusting for accuracy. On the other hand, gripping the cue too far away from the white ball can also cause a short movement of the cue and affect the accuracy of the shot. It is important to find a balance between the distance of the grip from the end of the cue and the distance from the white ball for optimal feathering.

Remember: When taking a shot, it is important to not lean the cue against your body, but instead, let your body lean against the cue. This will ensure that your shot is stable and accurate. By keeping your body in the proper position and allowing it to support the cue, you can reduce the chances of any unwanted movements that can affect the accuracy of your shot.

I believe that the handle of the cue is the core of the game. The shoulder joint is like the ball and it ultimately determines the position of the elbow. The elbow is a hinge joint, which means it only moves in one plane. The wrist is multi-functional and works in harmony with the elbow joint. The control of the cue is achieved through the fleshy network of skin between the thumb and forefinger. When gripping the cue, I hinge it with my index finger and close it with my thumb. This is the fundamental aspect of the grip. Then, by closing the other fingers and moving the cue forward and backward, I can develop the control of these fingers. The fingers should always be in contact with the cue during the shot.



Proper finger development is crucial when pulling back the cue to ensure that the cue is guided parallel to the table. If the fingers are not developed, the cue will swing and the shot will not be accurate. Furthermore, if a grip is used without developing the fingers, the body will engage additional muscles and activate the shoulder joint, which can lead to mistakes. It's important to work on finger development to make sure that the cue is controlled and guided parallel to the table, without the need to use additional muscles.


It's important to ensure that at the moment of contact between the cue tip and the cue ball, the forearm and cue form a right angle. This position aligns the cue most parallel to the table. If the cue is pulled back with the tip pointing upwards, it will not be parallel to the table at the moment of contact and can negatively impact the shot's accuracy. To have the best shot outcome, the cue should be parallel to the table at the moment of contact with the cue ball.

Forming a proper bridge is important for accurate shots. Here's how to do it:
1. Place your palm flat on the table, spreading your fingers to the desired width.
2. Carefully lift your knuckles by pulling the pads of your fingers across the table, making sure that the entire pads are resting on the surface.
3. Keep your fingers straight as you slide your thumb into the middle of your fingers and lock it onto your index finger. This will provide stability for the bridge.
4. The final result should be an indentation that resembles a letter V, through which the cue can pass. This will provide a stable base for the cue and allow for precise shots.

Bridge on the edge of the table.

When the cue ball is close to the cushion and a traditional bridge is not possible, there are alternative bridge techniques that can be used. One option is the "mechanic's bridge", where the base of the bridge hand is positioned on the cushion and the fingers support the cue. Another option is the "fingertip bridge", where the fingertips of the bridge hand are used to support the cue, rather than the base of the hand. A third option is the "rail bridge" where the cue is rested on the rail of the pool table instead of the bridge hand. These alternative bridges can be used to make a shot when a traditional bridge is not possible due to the position of the cue ball.

To establish a proper stance while playing snooker, it's important to first determine your firing line, which is the line that your cue will follow during the shot. To do this, align your right leg with the line of the shot, as this is the leg that will hold the cue. Your left leg should be positioned slightly wider than your hips to provide added stability. To increase longitudinal stability, which is stability along the line of the shot, move your left leg forward about 30cm and bend it slightly at the knee. Your right leg should remain straight. This stance will provide comfort and stability when striking the white ball.

Why the cue action?

The cue action is the deliberate and controlled movement of the cue in order to make the shot. It is important to keep the cue action calm, so that your motor skills are relaxed and the shot is executed smoothly. This will help to ensure that the cue ball is hit as precisely as possible.

Why the pause?

The pause before the shot is crucial to maintaining control and accuracy. Without a pause, the cue may accelerate too quickly upon impact, causing the shot to be executed poorly. This can lead to a lack of control and difficulty in keeping the cue in a straight line. Pausing before the shot will help to maintain control and ensure a smooth and accurate execution.

Where do you look when hitting the white ball? When striking the white ball, you should look at the ball that you are trying to hit. It is not recommended to look at the ball that has just been played (the cue ball) as this can affect your aim and accuracy.

This can be done in three ways:

The first way - We prepare to strike and look at the white ball and the ball being played, keeping our eyes focused on the cue as it moves back and forth. We make sure to watch the cue as it moves along the line of impact. This method allows us to see how the cue is moving and adjust the strike if necessary. However, it takes more focus to keep our eyes on the cue instead of the ball being played.

The second way - We prepare to strike by keeping our eyes on the white ball and the ball to be struck. We focus on the point of impact and make the strike without looking at the cue as it retreats. This method allows for quick and precise strikes, but it can be difficult to gauge the power of the shot without visually tracking the cue.

The third way - We prepare to strike by looking at the cue ball and aiming, then we focus on the cue as it moves back and forth. We make sure to watch the cue as it stops before the strike, then we look at the ball being played and strike. This method allows for precision and control of the cue, as well as giving us ample time to focus on the ball being played.

Where to position the dominant eye and where to hold the head during impact?

When the players fold up to hit and start the cue action, you may notice that some players cue more under the right or left eye. This is because they have one eye more dominant, so if the right eye is dominant cue will be more under the right eye.

To determine which eye is dominant, follow these steps:
1. Place a ball on a table.
2. Cut a hole in a piece of paper and hold it in front of your straightened arms.
3. Look through the hole at the ball and close your left eye. If you can still see the ball, your right eye is dominant.
4. Repeat the process, but this time close your right eye. If the ball disappears, your left eye is dominant.

How to hit the white ball on the played ball so that it falls into the pocket. How to choose the right angle for pocketing? This is the most common question asked by novice snooker players how to properly pocket and how to recognize the suitable angles.

If we have a play of the white cue ball at an angle to the cue ball played and the pocket, we can approach the strike in two ways ways.

The first way is to practice hitting angles where the cue ball and the ball played meld into a full ball (that is hitting straight), on 3/4 the ball, 1/2 the ball or 1/4 the ball. After practicing these angles we will begin to recognize whether the stroke is on 1/2 the ball or maybe on 3/4 the ball and by making slight slight adjustment we will be able to pocket the ball into the pocket.


The second way is to stand in line of the ball being played and pocket imagining the ball tangent to the ball played on the line leading to the pocket. Such ball is called a "ghost ball." Now without taking our eyes off the the imaginary ball we assemble to shot aiming perfectly at the center of the "ghost ball".



The best way to improve efficiency or simply learning is to combine the two methods. That is, combine regular practice of hammering from several angles, for example, at 20 times from each angle and, before each hit, imagine a ghost ball. Such a combination gives us a lot of information feedback and over time we will automatically and intuitively correctly fold in the right angle for a shot. When learning angles the most important thing is routine and repetition because this is how we way we acquire and consolidate both motor and memory information and memory information of recognizing and inserting from angles.

Stressful situations are the inseparable companion of every competitor, especially when a particular shot is decisive. Sometimes a seemingly simple shot can turn out to be difficult due to the weight of the shot. We perceive stress as a feeling of discomfort in a particular situation, understood as a state of tension. Tense neck and shoulder muscles is our body's reaction to stress, which makes it very difficult to perform a correct strike.

Stressors activate complex response mechanisms both physiological and psychological. It begins to secrete hormones of stress, such as cortisol, as well as adrenaline which, in the case of a game like is snooker is not advisable. Snooker player during the stroke should as calmly as possible bring out the hit so as not to introduce unwanted additional body movements.


How to deal with stress?

The first and main way to cope with stress is to develop, practice the best possible technique. Technique is the foundation of our game, which makes us better than another player in a stressful situation. To develop a good technique you need to devote hours a day to train technique.
The second way is breathing exercises. By a few seconds before an important impact, focus on your own breathing, take a deep inhale and then slowly let the air out this will cause your body will begin to relax and your breathing will regulate itself.
The third way equally important is visualization, the idea is that before each stroke you should be able to visualize your stroke as the cue ball hits the ball being played, which falls into the pocket while the cue ball stops in the position as planned.
